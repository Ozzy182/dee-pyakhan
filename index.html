Living Heritage

The Sacred Tradition

Kilagha Dee Pyākhan — also known as Dee Pyakhan or Dya Pyakhan — is an ancient masked deity dance performed exclusively in the historic neighborhood of Kilagha in Kathmandu, Nepal. Rooted in the devotional traditions of the Newar community, this sacred performance is enacted each year during Indra Jatra (Yenya), the grandest festival of Kathmandu Valley.

Kilagha — The Sacred Quarter

Nestled in the heart of old Kathmandu, Kilagha is one of the city's most revered Newar localities. Its ancient courtyards, elaborately carved temples, and community guthi halls have sheltered this dance tradition for centuries, making it inseparable from the quarter's identity.

Jyapu Newars — Guardians of Culture

The Jyapu — the farming community within the Newar ethnic group — are the traditional custodians of Dee Pyākhan. Their guthi (community institution) organises, funds, and perpetuates the performance, ensuring that sacred costumes, music, and dance steps pass faithfully from father to son across generations.

Indra Jatra — The Grand Stage

Indra Jatra, known locally as Yenya, is an eight-day festival honouring Indra, god of rain, and the departed ancestors. Dee Pyākhan is performed on specific auspicious days, its procession winding through Kilagha's narrow lanes as drums echo off centuries-old brick facades and marigold garlands fill the air.

Ritual & Spiritual Meaning

Every element of Dee Pyākhan carries deep meaning — the masks are consecrated before dawn, the costumes are worn ritually, and the performers undergo purification rites. The dance is not entertainment; it is puja in motion — an act of devotion where human bodies become temporary vessels for the divine.

"When the masks are worn and the dhime drums strike, it is not a man who dances — it is the deity itself that moves through the lanes of Kilagha."

— Elder of the Kilagha Guthi

The Divine Cast

Deities & Characters

Six sacred figures manifest through the dance — each a window into the vast Newar cosmological universe.

Bhairav

भैरव — The Wrathful Guardian

Bhairav

The supreme deity of the Dee Pyākhan, Bhairav is the fierce manifestation of Shiva. His terrifying mask — adorned with bulging eyes, fangs, and a crown of skulls — embodies cosmic power that protects Kilagha. He leads the procession, radiating divine wrath that purifies the path.

Lead Deity

Kumari

कुमारी — The Living Goddess

Kumari

Representing the living goddess tradition of Nepal, Kumari embodies the pre-pubescent divine feminine. Her serene golden mask contrasts with Bhairav's ferocity, symbolising grace and benevolent power. Her presence in the dance sanctifies all who witness it.

Divine Feminine

Chandi

चण्डी — The Fierce Warrior

Chandi

Chandi — the fierce, multi-armed warrior goddess — appears as the slayer of demons. In the dance, her dynamic movements represent the cosmic battle between order and chaos. Her wild energy and powerful gestures are considered particularly potent in warding off evil.

Warrior Goddess

Daitya

दैत्य — The Demon

Daitya

The Daitya represents the demonic forces of the universe — necessary counterparts to the divine. In Dee Pyākhan, the demon's exaggerated mask and lumbering dance create comic terror, delighting crowds while carrying the deeper message that darkness must be confronted and overcome.

Cosmic Antagonist

Khwoh

कवः — The Skeletal Spirit

Khwoh

Kawah (also called Kawan or Kawanchā) is a skeletal spirit figure found in Newar mythology and sacred masked dance traditions. In folklore, Kawah exists as the opposite or twin counterpart of Khyah — representing bone, death, the spirit realm, and the hidden forces that exist beyond ordinary life.

SKELETAL SPIRIT

Beta

बेत — The Sacred Attendant

Beta

Beta is the attendant and companion figure in the dance — a half-divine, half-human character who bridges the mortal and sacred worlds. Often depicted with an expressive, almost comedic mask, Beta interacts with spectators, making the divine procession accessible and joyful to all who watch.

Divine Attendant

Khya

ख्या — The Spirit

Khya

Khya is a spirit entity from Newar mythology — ethereal, unpredictable, and dwelling between worlds. In the procession, the Khya character moves with an otherworldly fluidity that distinguishes it from the deities. It serves as a reminder of the spirit world's omnipresence in Newar cosmology.

Spirit Entity

During Indra Jatra

Festival Journey

From pre-dawn preparations to the final offering — follow the sacred arc of Dee Pyākhan.

Pre-Dawn · 4:00 AM

Purification & Consecration

Before the city wakes, guthi members gather at the aganchhen (the sacred preparation hall). Masks are brought out, ritually bathed in fragrant water, and consecrated with mantras. Performers undergo fasting and purification rites — transforming from men into vessels of the divine.

Morning · 7:00 AM

Costuming & Masking

The elaborate costumes — silks, brocades, and traditional Newar fabrics — are donned with ritual care. The most sacred moment: when the mask is placed over the performer's face, he enters a meditative state, surrendering his individual self so the deity may inhabit him. A priest offers final blessings.

Midday · 12:00 PM

The Dhime Awakens

The great dhime drum begins its sacred rhythm, and the naykhī (cymbals) answer in call and response. The sound is said to carry prayers to the heavens. Spectators fill the ancient lanes; marigold petals and red powder are scattered as the dancers begin their preliminary movements in the inner courtyard.

Afternoon · 3:00 PM

The Grand Procession

Bhairav leads the procession out into the streets of Kilagha, followed by the full cast of deities and characters. The route is ancient and fixed — winding past shrines, beneath carved torana gateways, and through the heart of the community. Each stop corresponds to a shrine where offerings are made and blessings bestowed.

Evening · 6:30 PM

Performance at the Square

As evening falls, the dancers gather at the main tol (open square) for the centrepiece performance. Oil lamps are lit along the edges. The full choreography unfolds — each deity enacting their mythological story in stylised movement, speaking to the community through gesture and dance vocabulary passed down for centuries.

Night · 9:00 PM

Unmasking & Gratitude

The final act is the ritual unmasking — done with prayers and offerings, formally releasing the deity's presence from the performer's body. The masks are then wrapped in sacred cloth and stored until next year's Indra Jatra. The guthi gathers for a communal meal, celebrating another year of sacred duty fulfilled.

The Sound of the Sacred

Music & Instruments

Traditional Newar music is the heartbeat of Dee Pyākhan — inseparable from movement and meaning.

Dhime

The dhime is the principal ritual drum of the Newar community — a large two-headed drum carried horizontally and beaten with a curved stick. Its deep, resonant boom is the pulse that guides the deities' movements and announces the sacred procession through the streets.

Naykhī

The naykhī (large cymbals) provide the rhythmic accent that gives Newar devotional music its characteristic shimmer. Clashed together in call-and-response with the dhime, they mark key moments in the choreography and signal transitions between scenes in the sacred dance narrative.

Muralī

The muralī (Newar flute) weaves melodic lines above the percussion bed — evoking the lightness of the divine and the poetic quality of Newar music. Its notes guide the softer, lyrical sections of the dance, particularly those embodying Kumari's serene presence.

Pūngi Bāja

Various pūngi bāja (traditional wind instruments) add harmonic texture to the ensemble. Together with percussion, they create the complete sonic landscape of Dee Pyākhan — a sound that can only be experienced in the streets of Kilagha during those charged evenings of Indra Jatra.

For Tomorrow

Preserving the Living Tradition

A Tradition at the Crossroads

Like many intangible cultural heritages, Kilagha Dee Pyākhan faces the pressures of modernity. Urban migration draws younger Jyapu Newars to distant cities; the intricate knowledge of dance movements, musical compositions, and ritual protocols risks being lost between generations. The masks gather dust in halls where once they danced.

Yet the tradition endures — because of community.

The Guthi System

The traditional guthi — a uniquely Newar communal institution — remains the living backbone of preservation. Members contribute time, resources, and knowledge to maintain the tradition. Annual gatherings, apprenticeship of young performers, and collective decision-making ensure the dance cannot belong to any single individual: it belongs to Kilagha.

Teaching the Young

Preservation begins with storytelling. Elders sit with grandchildren and describe the deities, their stories, and what each movement means. Young men begin learning the drum rhythms before they are old enough to wear the masks. This oral and embodied transmission — body to body, heartbeat to heartbeat — is the most powerful archive a culture can possess.

01

Documentation

Community-led video and audio documentation of performances, elders' testimonies, and ritual protocols for future generations.

02

Apprenticeship

Young men from Kilagha are formally mentored by senior performers in the guthi's structured apprenticeship programme.

03

School Outreach

Local schools include Dee Pyākhan in cultural curricula; performance demonstrations bring the tradition into classrooms.

04

Mask Conservation

Ancient masks undergo conservation treatment in partnership with Nepal's Department of Archaeology to preserve their sacred materials.

05

Community Fund

A community-managed cultural fund supports costume repair, instrument replacement, and the costs of annual performance.

06

Digital Archive

A growing digital archive of photographs, oral histories, and musical recordings is maintained by the Kilagha Guthi for open access.

"To preserve a culture is not to freeze it in amber — it is to give it roots deep enough to grow."
